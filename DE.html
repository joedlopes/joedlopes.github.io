Differential Evolution (DE)

A simple yet powerful population-based optimization algorithm.

Use the arrows on the left and right to navigate.

History

Differential Evolution was introduced by Rainer Storn and Kenneth Price in 1997.

They developed it as a heuristic method for optimizing real-valued functions, particularly for problems that are non-linear and non-differentiable.

Its foundation lies in the idea of using vector differences between population members to perturb and guide the search for an optimum.

Objective

The primary goal of Differential Evolution is to find the global optimum of a given function (the objective function).

It operates on a population of candidate solutions (called "agents" or "vectors") and iteratively tries to improve these solutions by applying evolutionary operators: mutation, crossover, and selection.

DE is designed to be robust and effective for a wide range of continuous optimization problems.

DE Algorithm Workflow

The core of DE follows a simple yet effective iterative process:

1. Initialize -> 2. Mutate -> 3. Crossover -> 4. Select -> 5. Repeat

What Problems Can It Solve?

DE is exceptionally versatile and has been applied to numerous domains:

  • Function Optimization: Finding the minimum or maximum of complex mathematical functions.
  • Machine Learning: Training neural networks and tuning hyperparameters.
  • Engineering Design: Optimizing designs for structures, circuits, and antennas.
  • Financial Modeling: Portfolio optimization and forecasting.
  • Data Science: Feature selection and clustering.

Advantages

  • Simplicity: The core algorithm is easy to understand and implement (often in just a few lines of code).
  • Few Control Parameters: Typically only requires setting the population size (NP), crossover rate (CR), and mutation factor (F).
  • Efficiency: Often finds a good solution in a relatively small number of function evaluations.
  • Robustness: Performs well on a wide variety of optimization landscapes.

Disadvantages

  • Parameter Tuning: Performance can be sensitive to the choice of NP, CR, and F, which may require tuning for a specific problem.
  • Premature Convergence: Can sometimes get stuck in local optima, especially on very complex or deceptive problems.
  • Discrete Variables: The classic DE is designed for continuous domains and requires modification to handle discrete or integer variables.

Core Operator: Mutation

Mutation is the key operator in DE for generating new candidate solutions and driving exploration.

It creates a "mutant vector" $v_i$ by combining several existing vectors from the population. The most common mutation strategies involve calculating a scaled difference between two vectors and adding it to a third.

The scaling factor, F, controls the amplification of the differential variation.

Variable Definitions

This table defines the variables used in the upcoming equations.

Variable Description
$x_{i,G}$ The target vector (individual $i$) from the current generation $G$.
$v_{i,G+1}$ The mutant vector, created for individual $i$ for the next generation.
$u_{i,G+1}$ The trial vector, created after crossover for individual $i$.
$G$ The current generation number.
$F$ The mutation factor or scaling factor (usually between 0 and 2).
$CR$ The crossover rate (probability, between 0 and 1).
$r1, r2, ...$ Indices of random, distinct individuals from the population.
$f(x)$ The objective function to be minimized.

Mutation Strategy: de/rand/1

This is the most classic and widely used DE mutation strategy. It adds the scaled difference of two random vectors to a third random vector.

Equation:
$$v_{i,G+1} = x_{r1,G} + F \cdot (x_{r2,G} - x_{r3,G})$$

Where $r1, r2, r3$ are distinct random indices, $F$ is the mutation factor, and $G$ is the current generation.

Mutation Strategy: de/best/1

This strategy uses the population's best vector so far as the base vector. This increases exploitation and can speed up convergence.

Equation:
$$v_{i,G+1} = x_{best,G} + F \cdot (x_{r1,G} - x_{r2,G})$$

Where $x_{best,G}$ is the fittest individual in the current generation.

Mutation Strategy: de/current-to-best/1

This is a rotational-invariant strategy that combines the current vector with information about the best vector and a random difference vector.

Equation:
$$v_{i,G+1} = x_{i,G} + F \cdot (x_{best,G} - x_{i,G}) + F \cdot (x_{r1,G} - x_{r2,G})$$

This approach often provides a good balance between exploration and exploitation.

Mutation Strategy: de/best/2

This strategy uses two difference vectors, which can increase population diversity and help escape local optima.

Equation:
$$v_{i,G+1} = x_{best,G} + F \cdot (x_{r1,G} - x_{r2,G}) + F \cdot (x_{r3,G} - x_{r4,G})$$

It's generally more exploratory than `de/best/1`.

Mutation Strategy: de/rand/2

Similar to `de/best/2`, this strategy also uses two difference vectors but with a random base vector, further enhancing exploration.

Equation:
$$v_{i,G+1} = x_{r1,G} + F \cdot (x_{r2,G} - x_{r3,G}) + F \cdot (x_{r4,G} - x_{r5,G})$$

This is one of the most explorative strategies available.

Core Operator: Crossover

Crossover (or recombination) builds a "trial vector" $u_i$ by mixing components from the original vector $x_i$ and the mutant vector $v_i$.

The most common method is Binomial Crossover. For each dimension (gene), a random number is compared to the Crossover Rate (CR). If it's smaller, the gene is taken from the mutant vector; otherwise, it's taken from the original vector.

Logic:
$$u_{i,j,G+1} = \begin{cases} v_{i,j,G+1} & \text{if } \text{rand}_j \le \text{CR or } j = j_{\text{rand}} \\ x_{i,j,G} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

A "guaranteed" crossover at index $j_{rand}$ ensures the trial vector is not an exact copy of the original.

Core Operator: Selection

Selection is the final step. It's a simple, greedy process: the newly created trial vector $u_i$ is evaluated and compared to the original vector $x_i$.

The one with the better (lower, for minimization) fitness value survives and becomes part of the population for the next generation.

Greedy Selection:
$$x_{i,G+1} = \begin{cases} u_{i,G+1} & \text{if } f(u_{i,G+1}) \le f(x_{i,G}) \\ x_{i,G} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
